Axios gets you smarter, faster with news and information that matters. We redesigned Axios.com for search engine optimization, to improve our revenue and traffic.

The Problem

I joined Axios right as the company kicked off a website redesign in time for its third birthday. Leadership had just approved the team’s overall website redesign strategy: improve the experience of our “engaged users” – only about 1% of Axios’ audience of eight million, but also the most likely to convert to our other products (newsletters and a soon-to-be-launched app).

Axios 1.0: An infinite feed of "atomic units" (the two paragraph story summary signature of Axios' writing style) and native advertisements.

Situational Awareness

As the sole designer on the project, I led our user discovery research to help contextualize our work. We interviewed seven engaged users (users who had frequently visited Axios.com directly) and four unengaged users (users who had visited an Axios story via a “side door” like social or search) to learn more about their general digital behavior and media consumption habits, and their impressions and use of Axios.com.

Through this process, we validated that engaged users’ main “Jobs To Be Done” on Axios.com are: situational awareness (to catch up on what Axios thinks matters now) and news analysis (to read Axios’ coverage of and take on a topic). We further segmented engaged users into four groups based on their motivations and behavior (see below). While our existing site best served the “FOMO-er” – someone who values frictionless and constant consumption of the news – addressing the other three segments offered an opportunity for the redesign.

Why It Matters

The website redesign became the design team’s most public facing project. To include stakeholders early in the redesign process, I led ideation sessions with different groups (editorial, growth, client partnerships) based on the user stories I created from the research.

In our ideation process, I also found inspiration from products intended for habitual use. The Calm meditation app, for example, extends its brand of “calmness” through the user journey – surfacing the most important content and then offering the user a sense of completion, and measuring success by sessions completed and sessions per week rather than time spent in the app. How might Axios similarly extend our brand of “trustworthiness” to the user journey?

Reality Check

Halfway through the project, however, our design priorities changed; we discovered that company leadership and our product team disagreed about the impetus for the redesign. It turned out that SEO (and relatedly, ad revenue), rather than user experience, was the most important factor driving the redesign from the top. We pivoted from our engaged users to our unengaged users – focusing on growing our “side door” traffic from social and search. In addition to this mid-project shift, we received a hard deadline to launch the redesigned site in three months, in time for Axios’ third anniversary.

Our biggest challenge became balancing user needs with business needs (SEO and advertising) in a short time frame. Designing for SEO and designing for our users sometimes aligned: we rethought the site’s information architecture to reflect users’ mental models with the help of a card sorting exercise, traded an infinite scroll for pagination, and added a content archive. But we also made compromises in our design decisions: to the dismay of our most loyal users (defined as the “FOMO-er” in our research), we replaced our unique inline story unfurl with a more traditional full story page, and we prioritized the mobile experience over desktop.

The Bottom Line

Given that our new site – especially the MVP version – caters to SEO and side door traffic, our most engaged users lamented some of the changes made. If we had worked with a longer timeline, we could have eased the transition by preparing our audience with a more transparent and gradual roll out of the new design. Ultimately though, the success of the redesign (from the perspective of our company) is measured by Google’s response to it.

What's Next

Now that we’ve launched the MVP of Axios’ new site, we have the time to develop our earlier user research findings, to define UX metrics for the site, and to user test future features. As we continue to iterate, we hope to find a better equilibrium between user needs (the experience of engaged users) and business needs (SEO, advertising, side door traffic).

Early SEO metrics are promising: we increased search traffic from 12% of total traffic to 20% within a month. Within two months, we increased our baseline of 75,000 daily Google-referred sessions on Axios.com to 400,000 sessions on a given day, a 5x increase. We have also experienced our seven biggest traffic days (with a record of 2.81 million sessions in a day) in the history of the company.
