Axios gets you smarter, faster with news and information that matters. We redesigned Axios.com for search engine optimization, to improve our revenue and traffic.

The Problem

I joined Axios right as the company kicked off a website redesign in time for its third birthday. Leadership had just approved the team’s overall website redesign strategy: improve the experience of our “engaged users” – only about 1% of Axios’ audience of eight million, but also the most likely to convert to our other products (newsletters and a soon-to-be-launched app). I set out to design a site that answers engaged users’ unmet needs, serves as the top of the conversion funnel into our products, and introduces Axios’ mission and new brand identity to users.

Situational Awareness

As the sole designer on the project, I led our user discovery research to help contextualize our future work. We interviewed seven engaged users (users who had frequently visited axios.com directly) and four unengaged users (users who had visited an Axios story via a “side door” like social or search) to learn more about their general digital behavior and media consumption habits, and their impressions and use of Axios.com.

Through this process, we validated that engaged users’ main “Jobs To Be Done” on Axios.com are: situational awareness (to catch up on what Axios thinks matters now) and news analysis (to read Axios’ coverage of and take on a topic). We further segmented engaged users into four groups based on their motivations and behavior (see below). While our existing site best served the “FOMO-er” – someone who values frictionless and constant consumption of the news – addressing the other three segments offered an opportunity for the redesign.

Why It Matters

The website redesign became the design team’s most public facing project, and thus most sensitive to feedback. To include stakeholders early in the redesign process, I led ideation sessions with different groups (editorial, growth, client partnerships) based on the user stories I created from the research.

In our ideation process, I also found inspiration from products intended for habitual use. The Calm meditation app, for example, extends its brand of “calmness” through the user journey – surfacing the most important content and then offering the user a sense of completion, and measuring success by sessions completed and sessions per week rather than time spent in the app. How might Axios similarly extend our brand of “trustworthiness” to the user journey?

Axios homepage: We leaned into Axios' mission to create news worthy of readers' time and attention. Users can view the homepage as a curated list of "big things" that matter now (the Batcher, the Deep Reader), or all of Axios' top stories (the FOMO-er). Each story is displayed as its "atomic unit" – the two paragraph summary signature of Axios' writing style.

Axios homepage: We recommended changing our existing site's infinite scroll to a paginated feed. This small moment of friction can discourage mindless scrolling and encourage more intentional news consumption – something users identified as important to them.

Story modal: If a user wanted to read a full story on our existing site, the story expanded ("unfurled") inline, in the context of an infinite news feed. Our user research found that users tend to separate browsing the news from deep reading of the news, especially valuing the latter. We suggested changing the existing interaction to a modal, to encourage a sense of focus and completion, while not losing the seamless and fast transition that the unfurl offered.

Section page: Section and subsection pages follow the same paradigm as the homepage – curating the most important things from everything else. The "big things" curation at the top of these pages offers our editorial team a way to highlight and resurface our evergreen content – a priority for our editorial team, and also a way to signal to users that Axios specializes in in-depth analysis in addition to its more well-known of-the-moment reporting.

Reality Check

Halfway through the project, however, our design priorities changed; we discovered that company leadership and our product team disagreed about the impetus for the redesign. It turned out that SEO (and relatedly, ad revenue), rather than user experience, was the most important factor driving the redesign from the top. We pivoted from our engaged users to our unengaged users – focusing on growing our “side door” traffic from social and search. In addition to this mid-project shift, we received a hard deadline to finish the design and development of the site in three months, in time for Axios’ third anniversary.

Our biggest challenge became balancing user needs with business needs (SEO and advertising) in a short time frame. Designing for SEO and designing for our users sometimes aligned: we rethought the site’s information architecture to reflect users’ mental models with the help of a card sorting exercise, traded an infinite scroll for pagination, and added a content archive. But we also made compromises in our design decisions: much to the dismay of our most loyal users (defined as the “FOMO-er” in our research), we replaced our unique inline story unfurl with a more traditional full story page, and we prioritized the mobile experience over desktop.

The Bottom Line

Given that our new site – especially the MVP version – caters to SEO and side door traffic, our most engaged users lamented many of the changes we had made. If we had worked with a longer timeline, we could have eased the transition by preparing our audience with a more transparent and gradual roll out of the new design. Ultimately though, the success of the redesign (from the perspective of our company) will be measured by Google’s response to it.

What's Next

Now that we’ve launched the MVP of Axios’ new site, we have the time and space to return to our earlier user research findings and design principles. As we continue to iterate, we hope to find a better equilibrium between user needs (the experience of engaged users) and business needs (SEO, advertising, side door traffic).
